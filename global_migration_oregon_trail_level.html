Teacher Implementation Guide
Global Migration Challenge
OVERVIEW
This activity is a comprehensive classroom simulation designed to be used in conjunction with your current immigration, cultural geography, or global studies unit. All simulation rules and activities have been tested in rural classroom settings and are designed to be engaging and educational. Students will experience the challenges, decisions, and cultural adaptations faced by immigrants throughout history while developing critical thinking, research, and communication skills.
This simulation integrates seamlessly with ELA (primary source analysis, journaling, vocabulary development), Science (population dynamics, environmental adaptation, data analysis), and Social Studies (cultural geography, economics, civics) standards for grades 6-8.
SAMPLE DAY
What does a classroom day look like during the Global Migration Challenge? Below is a sample explanation:
Today we are departing from Ireland during the Potato Famine (1847) and traveling to Ellis Island, New York. This journey typically took 6-8 weeks by ship and cost approximately $15-20 per person (equivalent to $500-700 today). I discuss the historical context using primary source documents and images from our Ellis Island Immigration Collection.

After we've studied the historical background, I have a representative from each family group come forward and roll the dice using our interactive Journey Event Chart. Based on their roll, families may experience storms at sea, illness, food shortages, or favorable winds.

Students then record all necessary information on their Journey Tracking Sheets: money spent, supplies used, family health status, and calendar progression. I help them understand how the immigration process worked and what challenges families faced.

After the dice roll, we might process through Ellis Island inspection, analyze primary source immigration documents, practice English language skills, or return to our regular social studies curriculum.
IDEAS FOR IMPLEMENTATION
Below are tested classroom strategies you may want to incorporate:
  • Character Development: I typically take a full day to have students develop their immigrant family stories: country of origin, reasons for leaving, family composition, occupations, and dreams for America. Groups then share their stories with the class.
  • Historical Context: I provide 20 minutes for students to research their country of origin using provided primary sources and historical background materials before beginning the journey.
  • Journey Timing: I like to start the simulation with departure from the home country because it allows students to experience the full immigration process from decision to settlement.
  • Resource Management: Families start with limited money and must make difficult decisions about what to bring, how to travel, and where to settle. This creates authentic decision-making scenarios.
  • Language Barriers: Partway through the simulation, I introduce language challenges where families must communicate basic needs without using English, simulating the immigrant experience.
  • Cultural Adaptation: Students must research and adapt to American customs, foods, and social expectations while maintaining their cultural identity.
  • Ellis Island Processing: I create a detailed Ellis Island inspection process where students must pass health, legal, and financial requirements to enter America.
  • Settlement Challenges: Once in America, families face housing, employment, and education challenges that require problem-solving and collaboration.
  • Journaling: As homework, students write detailed journal entries from their character's perspective, describing daily experiences, emotions, and observations about their new country.
  • Primary Source Integration: Students analyze actual immigration documents, letters home, and oral histories to understand the authentic immigrant experience.

OVERVIEW
The year is 1847-1920, and you are about to embark upon one of the most challenging and life-changing journeys in human history. Get ready for adventure, hardship, hope, and determination as you leave your homeland and travel to America seeking a better life. You will start in your country of origin, travel across the ocean, process through Ellis Island, and begin building a new life in America.

Family Background: You will be part of an immigrant family group with 4-5 members. Each family will determine their background by rolling dice:

CHARACTER DEVELOPMENT
Each family member must develop a detailed character profile:
  • Name: Research authentic names from your country and time period
  • Age: Determine realistic ages for family members
  • Relationship: Father, mother, child, grandparent, cousin, etc.
  • Occupation: What did you do in the old country?
  • Reason for Leaving: Why is your family immigrating?
  • Dreams for America: What do you hope to achieve?
  • Special Skills: Languages, trades, talents that might help
FAMILY RESPONSIBILITIES
Each family member will have specific duties to help track your immigration journey:
  • Money Manager: Track all financial transactions, expenses, and earnings
  • Supply Keeper: Monitor possessions, luggage, and important documents
  • Health Monitor: Track family health, medical needs, and nutrition
  • Journey Recorder: Maintain calendar, locations, and travel progress
  • Cultural Liaison: Research customs, language, and adaptation needs
THE IMMIGRATION JOURNEY
Phase 1: Departure Preparation
Before leaving your homeland, you must make critical decisions about what to bring, how to travel, and how to prepare for the journey. You have limited money and space, so choose wisely!
Phase 2: Ocean Crossing
The journey to America takes 6-12 weeks depending on weather, ship conditions, and your class of travel. Each week, you'll roll dice to determine what happens during your voyage. You may face storms, illness, food shortages, or make new friends.
Phase 3: Ellis Island Processing
Upon arrival in New York Harbor, you must pass through Ellis Island immigration inspection. This includes health examinations, legal questioning, and financial verification. Not everyone who arrives is allowed to enter America!
Phase 4: American Settlement
Once admitted to America, you must find housing, employment, and begin adapting to American culture while maintaining your family's cultural identity and traditions.

Immigration Journal Prompts
JOURNAL REQUIREMENTS
Throughout your immigration journey, you will maintain a detailed journal from your character's perspective. Each entry should be 1-2 paragraphs and include specific historical details, personal emotions, and authentic experiences based on your research and simulation events.
WEEKLY JOURNAL PROMPTS
Week 1 - Departure Preparation:
Write about your family's decision to leave your homeland. What events or conditions forced this difficult choice? Describe your emotions as you pack your few possessions and say goodbye to everything familiar. What are your fears and hopes for America?
Week 2 - Ocean Crossing Begins:
Describe your first week aboard the ship. What are the living conditions like? How do you spend your days? What other passengers have you met, and what are their stories? Include details about food, sleeping arrangements, and daily routines.
Week 3 - Mid-Journey Challenges:
Write about any difficulties your family has faced during the voyage. This might include illness, storms, conflicts with other passengers, or homesickness. How is your family coping with these challenges? What gives you strength to continue?
Week 4 - Cultural Connections:
Describe interactions with other immigrant families from different countries. What have you learned about their cultures, languages, and reasons for coming to America? How are their experiences similar to or different from yours?
Week 5 - Approaching America:
Write about your anticipation as you near America. What have you heard about Ellis Island and the inspection process? What are your concerns about being admitted to the country? Describe your preparations for arrival.
Week 6 - Ellis Island Processing:
Describe your experience going through Ellis Island inspections. Include details about the health examination, legal questioning, and any challenges your family faced. What were your emotions during this process? How did it feel to finally be admitted (or not) to America?
ADDITIONAL JOURNAL TOPICS
  • Language Barriers: Write about attempts to communicate in English and misunderstandings that occurred.
  • Cultural Traditions: Describe how your family maintains cultural practices while adapting to American ways.
  • Letters Home: Write a letter to relatives in your homeland describing your journey and new life.
  • American Dreams: Reflect on whether America is meeting your expectations and what you hope to achieve.
  • Helping Others: Describe times when you helped other immigrants or received help from strangers.
  • Discrimination: Write about any prejudice or unfair treatment your family experienced and how you responded.
JOURNAL ASSESSMENT CRITERIA
Your journal will be evaluated based on:
  • Historical Accuracy: Use of authentic details from research and primary sources
  • Character Development: Consistent voice and perspective throughout entries
  • Emotional Depth: Genuine reflection on the immigrant experience
  • Writing Quality: Clear expression, proper grammar, and engaging narrative
  • Cultural Understanding: Demonstration of knowledge about immigration history and cultural adaptation
