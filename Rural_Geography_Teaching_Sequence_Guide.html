Recommended Teaching Sequence
This sequence is designed to build geographic thinking skills progressively, starting with foundational Earth systems and advancing to complex human-environment interactions. Each simulation builds upon previous learning while introducing new concepts and skills.
UNIT 1: Earth Systems Explorer
Essential Question: How do Earth's physical systems create the foundation for all life and human activity?
Big Idea: Earth's interconnected systems (atmosphere, hydrosphere, geosphere, biosphere) create the environmental conditions that support and challenge human societies.
Why Start Here: Students need foundational understanding of physical geography before exploring human geography. This simulation establishes vocabulary, systems thinking, and scientific observation skills used throughout the sequence.
Key Standards: NGSS MS-ESS2-4 (water cycle), MS-ESS3-3 (human impact), CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.RST.6-8.7 (technical information)

UNIT 2: Amazon Expedition Challenge
Essential Question: How do scientists explore and understand Earth's most complex ecosystems?
Big Idea: Scientific exploration requires careful observation, data collection, and analysis to understand biodiversity and ecosystem interactions.
Why Second: Builds on Earth systems knowledge by focusing on one specific biome. Introduces scientific method, data collection, and primary source analysis skills. Alfred Russel Wallace's journals provide excellent ELA integration.
Prerequisites: Understanding of climate systems, water cycle, and basic ecosystem concepts from Unit 1.
‚Üì
UNIT 3: Global Migration Challenge
Essential Question: What forces drive human migration, and how do people adapt to new environments?
Big Idea: Human migration is driven by push and pull factors, and successful adaptation requires understanding both physical and cultural geography.
Why Third: Introduces human geography concepts while building on environmental knowledge. Students understand how physical geography influences human decisions. Rich primary sources develop ELA skills.
Prerequisites: Understanding of climate zones, environmental challenges, and basic data analysis from Units 1-2.

UNIT 4: Nation Builder Challenge
Essential Question: How do geographic factors influence the development of governments and societies?
Big Idea: Geographic features, resources, and location significantly impact how societies organize themselves politically and economically.
Why Fourth: Builds on migration concepts by exploring how settled populations create governments. Integrates civics education with geographic thinking. Federalist Papers provide advanced ELA content.
Prerequisites: Understanding of human-environment interaction, cultural adaptation, and basic economic concepts from Units 1-3.
‚Üì
UNIT 5: Global Trade Network Game
Essential Question: How do geographic factors create opportunities and challenges for global economic connections?
Big Idea: Geographic location, resources, and transportation networks determine patterns of global trade and economic interdependence.
Why Fifth: Synthesizes previous learning by exploring economic geography. Students apply understanding of physical geography, human migration, and political systems to economic relationships.
Prerequisites: Understanding of resource distribution, transportation challenges, cultural exchange, and basic government systems from Units 1-4.
‚Üì
UNIT 6: Silk Road Trader Challenge
Essential Question: How did historical trade networks connect distant civilizations and spread ideas across continents?
Big Idea: Long-distance trade networks overcome geographic barriers to create cultural, technological, and economic exchange between civilizations.
Why Sixth: Applies all previous concepts to historical context. Students use geographic knowledge to understand medieval trade, cultural diffusion, and technological innovation. Marco Polo's accounts provide rich primary sources.
Prerequisites: Comprehensive understanding of physical geography, human adaptation, political systems, and economic networks from Units 1-5.

UNIT 7: Trans-Saharan Adventure
Essential Question: How do extreme environments challenge human societies and create unique cultural adaptations?
Big Idea: Extreme geographic environments require specialized knowledge, technology, and cultural practices for human survival and prosperity.
Why Final: Culminating experience that challenges students to apply all geographic concepts in the most demanding environment. Ibn Battuta's accounts provide sophisticated primary source analysis. Synthesizes physical geography, cultural adaptation, trade networks, and survival strategies.
Prerequisites: Mastery of all previous concepts including environmental adaptation, navigation, trade economics, cultural exchange, and advanced primary source analysis.
Assessment Progression
Formative Assessment Throughout Sequence:
  • Units 1-2: Focus on scientific observation, data collection, and basic analysis
  • Units 3-4: Add primary source analysis, argumentative writing, and cultural comparison
  • Units 5-6: Integrate economic reasoning, systems thinking, and historical analysis
  • Unit 7: Synthesize all skills in complex, multi-faceted challenges
Summative Assessment Options:
  • Portfolio Assessment: Collection of work from all 7 simulations showing growth
  • Capstone Project: Student-designed simulation incorporating all geographic concepts
  • Comparative Analysis: Essay comparing challenges across different environments and time periods
  • Presentation Series: Students teach younger classes using simulation materials
Flexible Implementation
Full Year Implementation: One simulation per month (September-May), allowing 3-4 weeks per unit with review and assessment time.
Semester Implementation: Select 3-4 simulations based on curriculum priorities. Recommended core sequence: Earth Systems ‚Üí Global Migration ‚Üí Nation Builder ‚Üí Silk Road.
Quarter Implementation: Choose 2 simulations that best match existing curriculum. Earth Systems + one human geography simulation works well.

  • Units 5-7: CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.WHST.6-8.2 (explanatory writing), RST.6-8.9 (compare texts)

  • Units 1-2: MS-ESS2-4 (water cycle), MS-LS1-5 (environmental factors)
  • Units 3-4: MS-ESS3-3 (human impact), MS-ETS1-1 (design problems)

  • Human Geography: Units 3-5 explore human-environment interaction
  • Cultural Geography: Units 6-7 examine cultural exchange and adaptation
