üåü 7th Grade: Migration Patterns and Demographics

Lesson 1: Types of Migration

Objective: Students will classify different types of migration and analyze their causes.

üöö Migration Classifications:

  • Internal Migration: Moving within the same country
  • International Migration: Moving between countries
  • Voluntary Migration: Choosing to move for better opportunities
  • Forced Migration: Must move due to war, disaster, persecution
  • Temporary Migration: Moving for a limited time (seasonal work)
  • Permanent Migration: Moving with intention to stay

Lesson 2: Push and Pull Factors

Objective: Students will analyze push and pull factors that influence migration decisions.

‚¨ÖÔ∏è‚û°Ô∏è Push and Pull Factors:

  • Push Factors (why people leave):
    • Lack of jobs or low wages
    • Political persecution or war
    • Natural disasters
    • Poor living conditions
    • Limited educational opportunities
  • Pull Factors (what attracts people):
    • Job opportunities and higher wages
    • Political freedom and safety
    • Better living conditions
    • Educational opportunities
    • Family connections

Lesson 3: Demographic Transition

Objective: Students will examine how birth rates, death rates, and population growth change over time.

üìä Demographic Transition Model:

  • Stage 1: High birth rates, high death rates (stable population)
  • Stage 2: High birth rates, declining death rates (rapid growth)
  • Stage 3: Declining birth rates, low death rates (slowing growth)
  • Stage 4: Low birth rates, low death rates (stable population)
  • Factors: Healthcare, education, economic development, urbanization

üìà Activity: Country Comparison

Research demographic data for four countries in different stages of demographic transition. Create graphs showing birth rates, death rates, and population growth rates. Explain what factors influence each country's stage.

üìù Quick Check:

Explain why death rates typically decline before birth rates during demographic transition. Give three factors that influence this pattern.

Lesson 4: Alaska's Demographic Characteristics

Objective: Students will analyze Alaska's unique demographic patterns and their implications.

üìã Alaska Demographics:

  • Age Structure: Younger than national average, many working-age adults
  • Gender Ratio: More males than females (especially in rural areas)
  • Ethnic Diversity: Large Alaska Native population, diverse immigrant groups
  • Urban vs Rural: Most live in Anchorage area, scattered rural communities
  • Mobility: High in-migration and out-migration rates

üéØ Activity: Demographic Profile Creation

üìù Assessment: Policy Implications

üåü 8th Grade: Global Population Challenges

Lesson 1: Global Population Distribution and Density

Objective: Students will analyze global patterns of population distribution and their causes.

üåç Global Population Patterns:

  • Population Clusters: East Asia, South Asia, Europe, Eastern North America
  • Sparse Areas: Polar regions, deserts, high mountains, dense forests
  • Carrying Capacity: Maximum population an environment can sustain
  • Ecumene: Permanently inhabited areas of Earth
  • Physiological Density: People per unit of arable land

üìù Quick Check:

Lesson 2: Migration Theory and Models

Objective: Students will apply migration theories and models to analyze population movements.

üìê Migration Models and Theories:

  • Gravity Model: Migration influenced by size and distance
  • Ravenstein's Laws: Principles governing migration flows
  • Life Course Migration: Age-related migration patterns
  • Chain Migration: Networks facilitating continued migration
  • Step Migration: Gradual movement through hierarchy of places

üî¨ Activity: Migration Model Testing

Test migration theories using Alaska data. Analyze migration flows between Alaska and other states, considering distance, population size, economic factors, and social networks. Present statistical analysis of which models best explain Alaska migration patterns.

üìù Quick Check:

Apply the gravity model to predict migration between Alaska and three other states. Explain how factors beyond distance and size might modify the basic gravity model prediction.

Objective: Students will evaluate population policies and their ethical implications.

‚öñÔ∏è Population Policy Approaches:

  • Anti-natalist Policies: Discourage high birth rates
  • Immigration Policies: Control international migration
  • Refugee Policies: Response to forced migration
  • Ethical Considerations: Human rights, cultural values, unintended consequences

üîÆ Future Population Trends:
