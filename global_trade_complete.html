üìö ELA Integration - Reading, Writing, Speaking & Listening

üìñ Primary Source Integration

Students read authentic trade documents, merchant correspondence, and economic treatises to understand global commerce through historical and contemporary perspectives, developing critical reading and business writing skills.

üìú Primary Source #1: Adam Smith's "The Wealth of Nations" (1776)
"The importation of gold and silver is not the principal much less the sole benefit which a nation derives from its foreign trade. Between whatever places foreign trade is carried on, they all of them derive two distinct benefits from it. It carries out that surplus part of the produce of their land and labor for which there is no demand among them, and brings back in return for it something else for which there is a demand. It gives a value to their superfluities by exchanging them for something else, which may satisfy a part of their wants, and increase their enjoyments."

- Adam Smith, The Wealth of Nations
Reading Comprehension Questions:
  1. According to Smith, what are the two main benefits of foreign trade?
  2. What does Smith mean by "surplus part of the produce"?
  3. How does trade "give value to their superfluities"?
  4. How does this principle apply to modern international trade?
üìú Primary Source #2: Mercantile System Critique (1776)
"The two great engines for enriching the country, therefore, were restraints upon importation, and encouragements to exportation. The importation of gold and silver is not the principal much less the sole benefit which a nation derives from its foreign trade. Consumption is the sole end and purpose of all production; and the interest of the producer ought to be attended to, only so far as it may be necessary for promoting that of the consumer."

- Adam Smith, critique of mercantilism
Analysis Questions:
  1. What were the "two great engines" of the mercantile system?
  2. Why does Smith argue that consumption should be the focus, not production?
  3. How does this critique apply to modern trade policies?
  4. What are the advantages and disadvantages of restricting imports?
üìú Primary Source #3: Hudson's Bay Company Trading Records (1775-1790)
"The trade in beaver pelts continues to be our most profitable venture, with demand remaining strong in European markets. However, we must carefully manage our relationships with indigenous trading partners, as their knowledge of the territory and trapping techniques is essential to our success. The cost of transportation from remote trading posts to our main depots represents a significant portion of our expenses, requiring careful calculation of profit margins on each shipment."

- Hudson's Bay Company Trading Records, circa 1780
Business Analysis Questions:
  1. What factors made beaver pelts profitable for the Hudson's Bay Company?
  2. Why were relationships with indigenous peoples crucial to the company's success?
  3. How did transportation costs affect the company's business model?
  4. What modern businesses face similar challenges with remote operations?

üî¨ Science Connections - Mathematics, Data Analysis & Geography

üìä Mathematical Approach to Trade Analysis

Students use mathematical reasoning, data analysis, and scientific methods to understand economic patterns, calculate business metrics, and analyze the geographic and technological factors that influence global trade.

üßÆ Mathematics Connections

Profit and Loss Calculations: Calculate revenue, expenses, and profit margins for trading operations. Use percentages to determine markup, discount rates, and return on investment.

Exchange Rate Mathematics: Convert currencies and calculate the impact of exchange rate fluctuations on international trade profits. Practice decimal operations and proportional reasoning.

Statistical Analysis: Analyze trade data using measures of central tendency, create graphs showing market trends, and interpret statistical information about global commerce.

üìà Data Analysis Connections

Supply and Demand Graphs: Create and interpret supply and demand curves, understanding how price changes affect quantity demanded and supplied in different markets.

Market Trend Analysis: Collect and analyze data on commodity prices, trade volumes, and economic indicators. Use spreadsheets to organize and visualize economic data.

Comparative Analysis: Compare economic performance between different trading companies, regions, or time periods using quantitative data and statistical methods.

üåç Geography and Transportation

Trade Route Analysis: Study how geographic features affect transportation costs and trade routes. Calculate distances, travel times, and shipping costs for different routes.

Climate and Agriculture: Analyze how climate patterns affect agricultural production and create opportunities for international trade in food products.

Resource Distribution: Map natural resource locations and understand how uneven distribution creates opportunities for international trade and economic specialization.

üíª Technology and Innovation

Transportation Technology: Study how improvements in ships, railroads, and airplanes have changed global trade patterns and reduced transportation costs.

Communication Systems: Analyze how telegraph, telephone, and internet technology have enabled faster business communication and more efficient trade.

Manufacturing Innovations: Examine how technological advances in production have created new trade opportunities and changed comparative advantages between countries.

8.F.B.5: Describe qualitatively the functional relationship between quantities (supply and demand)

6.SP.B.5: Summarize numerical data sets in relation to their context (trade statistics)

üìä Assessment & Evaluation

üìù Comprehensive Economic Learning Assessment

Assessment focuses on integrated understanding of economic principles, mathematical reasoning, and the ability to apply trade concepts to real-world business situations.

üéØ Integrated Assessment Rubric

ELA Integration (25%)

4 - Excellent: Effectively analyzes trade documents, writes compelling business proposals, uses economic vocabulary correctly

3 - Proficient: Analyzes documents well, writes clear business communications, uses most vocabulary correctly

2 - Developing: Basic document analysis, adequate business writing, some vocabulary errors

1 - Beginning: Limited analysis skills, unclear writing, frequent vocabulary errors

Mathematical Reasoning (25%)

4 - Excellent: Accurately performs complex calculations, interprets data effectively, uses math to support business decisions

3 - Proficient: Performs calculations correctly, understands basic data analysis

2 - Developing: Some calculation errors, basic understanding of mathematical concepts

1 - Beginning: Frequent calculation errors, limited mathematical reasoning

Economic Understanding (25%)

4 - Excellent: Demonstrates deep understanding of trade principles, supply and demand, and market dynamics

3 - Proficient: Shows good understanding of basic economic concepts and trade relationships

2 - Developing: Basic knowledge of economic principles, some understanding of trade

1 - Beginning: Limited understanding of economics and trade concepts

Business Skills (25%)

4 - Excellent: Demonstrates strong negotiation, planning, and decision-making skills

3 - Proficient: Shows good business judgment and communication skills

2 - Developing: Basic business skills, some effective decision-making

1 - Beginning: Limited business skills, poor decision-making
