üìç Alaska Social Studies Standards

Our curriculum is specifically designed to meet Alaska's unique educational requirements while connecting students to their local environment, culture, and heritage.

Middle School Geography

AK.SS.G.1: World in Spatial Terms

Integration: Students use maps, globes, and geographic tools to analyze Alaska's position in global contexts, comparing Alaska's geography with world regions studied.

Curriculum Example: When studying the Arctic, students compare Alaska's Arctic regions with those in Canada, Greenland, and Russia, analyzing how geography affects settlement patterns and economic activities.
Middle School Geography

AK.SS.G.2: Places and Regions

Integration: Students analyze the physical and human characteristics that define Alaska's regions and compare them with world regions.

Curriculum Example: Students examine how Alaska's climate regions (maritime, continental, Arctic) compare with similar regions worldwide, understanding how these similarities create shared challenges and opportunities.
Middle School Geography

AK.SS.G.3: Physical Systems

Integration: Students understand how physical processes shape Alaska's landscape and connect these to global physical systems.

Curriculum Example: When studying plate tectonics globally, students focus on the Pacific Ring of Fire's effects on Alaska, examining earthquake and volcanic activity in both Alaska and other Pacific Rim countries.
Middle School Geography

AK.SS.G.4: Human Systems

Integration: Students analyze human migration, settlement patterns, and cultural characteristics in Alaska and compare with global patterns.

Curriculum Example: Students trace migration patterns to Alaska (indigenous settlement, Russian colonization, gold rush, modern migration) and compare these with migration patterns in other world regions.

üåç Alaska Cultural Standards

Culturally Responsive Education

Alaska Cultural Standard A: Culturally-knowledgeable students are well grounded in the cultural heritage and traditions of their community

Integration: Every unit includes specific connections to Alaska Native cultures, traditions, and knowledge systems.

Curriculum Example: When studying Arctic regions globally, students learn traditional Alaska Native knowledge about sea ice, weather patterns, and seasonal changes, comparing this with indigenous knowledge from other Arctic peoples.
Culturally Responsive Education

Alaska Cultural Standard E: Culturally-knowledgeable students demonstrate an awareness and appreciation of the relationships and processes of interaction of all elements in the world around them

Integration: Students understand interconnections between Alaska ecosystems and global environmental systems.

Curriculum Example: Students examine how changes in Alaska's environment (melting permafrost, changing wildlife patterns) connect to global climate change, understanding local-global relationships.

üí° Why Alaska-Centered Education Matters

Research shows that students learn best when curriculum connects to their lives and experiences. By grounding world geography in Alaska examples, students:

  • Build deeper understanding by connecting new concepts to familiar places
  • Develop geographic thinking by seeing how local patterns connect to global systems
  • Appreciate their heritage by understanding Alaska's unique place in the world
  • Become global citizens who can contribute Alaska perspectives to worldwide conversations
