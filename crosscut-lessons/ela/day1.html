Argumentative Writing: Geographic Evidence

Cross-curricular connection: Students use geographic tools and data to support argumentative writing about land use decisions.

Subject: ELA
Grade: 6-8
Duration: 50 minutes
Integration: Social Studies (Geographic Tools)

üéØ Learning Objectives

  • Students will write argumentative claims supported by geographic evidence
  • Students will use maps, charts, and geographic data as evidence
  • Students will structure arguments using geographic reasoning
  • Students will evaluate the strength of geographic evidence in arguments

üìã Standards Alignment

Common Core ELA:

  • CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.WHST.6-8.1: Write arguments to support claims with clear reasons and relevant evidence
  • CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.RST.6-8.7: Integrate quantitative or technical information expressed in words in a text with a version of that information expressed visually

Alaska Social Studies:

  • Geography A.1: Use geographic tools to locate and analyze information about people, places, and environments

üìö Materials Needed

  • Local community maps (digital or printed)
  • Population density charts
  • Land use data for Alaska communities
  • Argumentative writing template
  • Sample geographic evidence examples
10 minutes

üöÄ Opening: Hook & Context

Scenario: Present students with a real dilemma: "The city of Fairbanks is considering expanding a shopping center into a wetland area. As community members, you need to write a letter to the city council arguing for or against this development."

Discussion: What kind of evidence would make your argument strongest? How can maps and geographic data help support your position?

15 minutes

üìñ Mini-Lesson: Geographic Evidence in Arguments

Teach:

  • Types of geographic evidence (maps, charts, data, satellite images)
  • How to read and interpret geographic data
  • Connecting geographic evidence to argumentative claims
  • Using geographic vocabulary in argumentative writing

Model: Show example argument using Alaska land use data with clear claim, evidence, and reasoning structure.

20 minutes

‚úçÔ∏è Guided Practice: Building Arguments with Maps

Activity: Students work with partners to:

  1. Analyze provided maps and data about the Fairbanks wetland scenario
  2. Identify at least 3 pieces of geographic evidence
  3. Write one strong argumentative paragraph using the evidence
  4. Use transition words and geographic vocabulary

Teacher Support: Circulate and provide feedback on evidence selection and argument structure.

5 minutes

üéØ Closing: Reflection & Preview

Exit Ticket: Students write one sentence explaining how geographic evidence made their argument stronger.

Preview: Tomorrow we'll learn how scientists use similar evidence-based reasoning to study Earth systems.

üìä Assessment

Formative: Monitor partner discussions and review exit tickets

Summative: Evaluate argumentative paragraphs using rubric focusing on:

  • Clear claim statement
  • Relevant geographic evidence
  • Logical reasoning connecting evidence to claim
  • Use of geographic vocabulary

üîÑ Differentiation

Support: Provide sentence frames and graphic organizers for argument structure

Extension: Advanced students research additional geographic data sources and evaluate evidence quality

ELL Support: Pre-teach geographic vocabulary with visual supports
