üî¨ Learning Activities

10 minutes

Opening: Alaska Weather Connection

  • Review ELA climate data essays from morning class
  • Discuss: "How can we use this data to predict weather patterns?"
  • Show satellite images of Alaska weather systems
  • Identify patterns students noticed in their writing
20 minutes

Weather System Modeling Activity

  • Students use physical models to demonstrate:
    • Air pressure differences (balloon demonstrations)
    • Temperature effects on air movement (hot/cold water circulation)
    • Earth's rotation effects (spinning platform with markers)
  • Record observations in scientific notebooks
  • Connect physical models to Alaska weather patterns
  • Use data from ELA essays to support observations
‚ö†Ô∏è Safety Note: Hot water demonstrations require teacher supervision. Use appropriate temperature water and safety equipment.
25 minutes

Digital Weather Modeling

  • Using available technology (or paper-based alternative):
    • Access weather modeling websites or use printed weather maps
    • Track pressure systems across North America
    • Predict Alaska weather based on approaching systems
    • Compare predictions with actual weather data
  • Create weather prediction models for 3 Alaska cities
  • Use evidence from both physical models and data analysis
  • Document reasoning using scientific vocabulary
15 minutes

Model Testing & Revision

  • Compare student predictions with actual weather outcomes
  • Identify factors that affected accuracy
  • Revise models based on new evidence
  • Discuss limitations of current models
  • Plan improvements for future predictions
5 minutes

Closing: Real-World Applications

  • Discuss how weather modeling helps Alaska communities
  • Connect to aviation, fishing, and subsistence activities
  • Preview tomorrow's lesson on climate change impacts
  • Assign reflection on modeling accuracy

üìä Assessment

Formative Assessment:

  • Scientific notebook observations during physical modeling
  • Participation in weather system discussions
  • Quality of reasoning in prediction explanations
  • Use of evidence from ELA data analysis

Summative Assessment:

  • Weather prediction model accuracy and reasoning (40%)
  • Scientific explanation of weather system causes (30%)
  • Integration of multiple data sources (20%)
  • Model revision based on evidence (10%)

Alaska Context Assessment:

  • Students can explain how Alaska's location affects its weather
  • Connect weather patterns to daily life in Alaska communities
  • Use local examples in scientific explanations

üè† Homework & Extension

  • Required: Track weather predictions vs. actual weather for 3 days
  • Extension: Research how Alaska Native communities traditionally predicted weather
  • Family Connection: Interview elder about weather pattern changes they've observed
  • Technology: If available, use weather apps to compare model accuracy

üéì Teacher Notes

  • Differentiation: Provide model templates for struggling students, challenge advanced students with multi-variable predictions
  • Technology Alternatives: Paper-based weather maps and data sheets if digital access limited
  • ELA Collaboration: Use student climate data essays to support scientific modeling
  • Rural Considerations: Emphasize local weather patterns relevant to student communities
  • Materials Needed: Balloons, hot/cold water, thermometers, weather maps, notebooks
  • Preparation: Review ELA essays beforehand, prepare local weather data

üìà Science & Engineering Practices

  • Modeling: Students create physical and digital weather models
  • Data Analysis: Interpret weather data patterns and trends
  • Evidence-Based Arguments: Support predictions with multiple data sources
  • Systems Thinking: Understand interactions between atmospheric components
  • Iteration: Revise models based on testing outcomes
