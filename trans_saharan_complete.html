üìñ Primary Source Integration

Students read authentic 14th-century travel accounts from Ibn Battuta, the famous Muslim explorer who crossed the Sahara and documented medieval African civilizations, developing critical reading and analytical writing skills.

üìú Primary Source #1: Ibn Battuta at the Salt Mines of Taghaza (1352)
"After twenty-five days [from Sijilmasa] we reached Taghaza, an unattractive village, with the curious feature that its houses and mosques are built of blocks of salt, roofed with camel skins. There are no trees there, nothing but sand. In the sand is a salt mine; they dig for the salt, and find it in thick slabs, lying one on top of the other, as though they had been tool-squared and laid under the surface of the earth. A camel will carry two of these slabs. No one lives at Taghaza except the slaves of the Massufa tribe, who dig for the salt; they subsist on dates imported from Dar'a and Sijilmasa, camels' flesh, and millet imported from the Negrolands. The negroes come up from their country and take away the salt from there. At Iwalatan a load of salt brings eight to ten mithqals; in the town of Malli [Mali] it sells for twenty to thirty, and sometimes as much as forty. The negroes use salt as a medium of exchange, just as gold and silver is used [elsewhere]; they cut it up into pieces and buy and sell with it."

- Ibn Battuta, Travels in Africa, 1352
Reading Comprehension Questions:
  1. What materials were used to build houses in Taghaza, and why?
  2. How did the salt trade create economic value across the Sahara?
  3. What does Ibn Battuta's description reveal about desert survival strategies?
üìú Primary Source #2: Ibn Battuta's Desert Crossing Experience
"We passed ten days of discomfort there, because the water is brackish and the place is plagued with flies. Water supplies are laid in at Taghaza for the crossing of the desert which lies beyond it, which is a ten-nights' journey with no water on the way except on rare occasions. We indeed had the good fortune to find water in plenty, in pools left by the rain. One day we found a pool of sweet water between two rocky prominences. We quenched our thirst at it and then washed our clothes. Truffles are plentiful in this desert and it swarms with lice, so that people wear string necklaces containing mercury, which kills them. At that time we used to go ahead of the caravan, and when we found a place suitable for pasturage we would graze our beasts. We went on doing this until one of our party was lost in the desert; after that I neither went ahead nor lagged behind. We passed a caravan on the way and they told us that some of their party had become separated from them. We found one of them dead under a shrub, of the sort that grows in the sand, with his clothes on and a whip in his hand. The water was only about a mile away from him."

- Ibn Battuta, Travels in Africa, 1352
Analysis Questions:
  1. What survival challenges did desert travelers face according to Ibn Battuta?
  2. How did caravans organize themselves for safety and survival?
  3. What does the story of the dead traveler teach us about desert dangers?
üìú Primary Source #3: Ibn Battuta Meets the King of Mali
"The sultan of Malli is Mansa Sulayman, 'mansa' meaning [in Mandingo] sultan, and Sulayman being his proper name. He is a miserly king, not a man from whom one might hope for a rich present. It happened that I spent these two months without seeing him, on account of my illness. Later on he held a banquet in commemoration of our master [the late sultan of Morocco] Abu'l-Hasan, to which the commanders, doctors, qadi and preacher were invited, and I went along with them. Reading-desks were brought in, and the Koran was read through, then they prayed for our master Abu'l-Hasan and also for Mansa Sulayman. When the ceremony was over I went forward and saluted Mansa Sulayman. The qadi, the preacher, and Ibn al-Faqih told him who I was, and he answered them in their tongue. They said to me, 'The sultan says to you Give thanks to God,' so I said, 'Praise be to God and thanks under all circumstances.' When I withdrew the [sultan's] hospitality gift was sent to me. It was taken first to the qadi's house, and the qadi sent it on with his men to Ibn al-Faqih's house. Ibn al-Faqih came hurrying out of his house barefooted, and entered my room saying, 'Stand up; here comes the sultan's stuff and gift to you.' So I stood up thinking--since he had called it 'stuff'--that it consisted of robes of honour and money, and lo!, it was three cakes of bread, and a piece of beef fried in native oil, and a calabash of sour curds. When I saw this I burst out laughing, and thought it a most amazing thing that they could be so foolish and make so much of such a paltry matter."

- Ibn Battuta, Travels in Africa, 1352
Contemporary Connection Questions:
  1. How does Ibn Battuta's cultural bias show in his description of the Mali king?
  2. What does this account reveal about different cultural values and gift-giving customs?
  3. How might a Mali historian describe this same meeting differently?

üî¨ Science Connections - Earth, Life & Physical Science

üåç Desert Ecosystem Science

Students explore how desert environments shape human adaptation, survival strategies, and trade patterns, while learning about climate systems, water cycles, and ecosystem interactions in arid environments.

üíß Water Science & Survival

Scientific Concepts: Desert survival depends on understanding water sources, conservation, and purification. Students investigate how medieval travelers found, stored, and conserved water during months-long desert crossings.

Investigation Questions:
  1. How do oases form in desert environments?
  2. What methods did medieval travelers use to purify brackish water?
  3. How does the human body adapt to extreme heat and water scarcity?
  4. What role do seasonal rains play in desert travel planning?

üß≠ Desert Navigation Science

Astronomical Navigation: Without landmarks, desert travelers relied on celestial navigation, understanding of prevailing winds, and knowledge of seasonal patterns. Students learn how medieval navigators used stars, sun positions, and natural indicators.

Hands-On Investigations:
  1. Practice celestial navigation using the North Star and constellation patterns
  2. Investigate how wind patterns create and move sand dunes
  3. Study how animals and plants indicate water sources in deserts
  4. Experiment with natural methods for determining direction

üå°Ô∏è Climate & Weather Systems

Desert Climate Science: The Sahara's extreme temperature variations, seasonal patterns, and weather systems determined when and how caravans could travel safely. Students analyze climate data and weather patterns.

Climate Analysis Activities:
  1. Graph daily temperature variations in desert environments
  2. Analyze seasonal rainfall patterns and their impact on travel
  3. Study how desert winds affect navigation and survival
  4. Investigate the formation and movement of sandstorms

üß™ Material Science & Preservation

Desert Chemistry: How did merchants preserve food, leather, and textiles in extreme heat and dry conditions? Students investigate salt preservation, natural desiccation, and material properties in desert environments.

Scientific Investigations:
  1. Test different food preservation methods in dry conditions
  2. Investigate the properties of salt as a preservative and currency
  3. Study how extreme temperatures affect different materials
  4. Experiment with natural methods for protecting goods from sand and heat

üê™ Animal Adaptation Science

Biological Adaptations: Camels, horses, and other animals were essential for desert travel. Students study animal adaptations to desert environments and how humans utilized these adaptations for survival and commerce.

Biological Investigations:
  1. Study camel adaptations for desert survival
  2. Compare water conservation strategies in desert animals
  3. Investigate how animals navigate in desert environments
  4. Analyze the carrying capacity of different pack animals

  • MS-ESS3-3: Apply scientific principles to design a method for monitoring and minimizing human impact on the environment
  • MS-LS1-5: Construct a scientific explanation based on evidence for how environmental and genetic factors influence the growth of organisms

üìö Activity Progression Overview

15 progressive activities building from basic desert geography concepts to complex analysis of medieval African civilizations, integrating ELA, science, and social studies throughout.

üó∫Ô∏è Activity 1: Desert Geography & Route Planning

Objective: Students map Trans-Saharan trade routes and identify key geographical features

Materials: Africa maps, colored pencils, geographical reference materials

Process:

  1. Trace major Trans-Saharan trade routes from North to West Africa
  2. Identify oases, salt mines, gold fields, and major trading cities
  3. Calculate distances and estimate travel times for caravans
  4. Analyze how geography influenced trade route development

Assessment: Accurate map with geographical analysis and route justification

üßÇ Activity 2: Salt-Gold Trade Economics

Objective: Understand the economic principles behind the salt-gold trade

Materials: Trade simulation materials, calculators, historical price data

Process:

  1. Research historical values of salt and gold in different regions
  2. Calculate profit margins for Trans-Saharan merchants
  3. Analyze supply and demand factors affecting trade
  4. Simulate trading negotiations between different cultural groups

Assessment: Economic analysis with mathematical calculations and reasoning

üìú Activity 3: Ibn Battuta Primary Source Analysis

Objective: Develop critical reading skills using authentic medieval travel accounts

Materials: Ibn Battuta excerpts, analysis worksheets, historical context materials

Process:

  1. Read Ibn Battuta's descriptions of Saharan travel and African kingdoms
  2. Identify cultural bias, perspective, and reliability issues
  3. Compare Ibn Battuta's accounts with archaeological evidence
  4. Write analytical essays on source interpretation and historical value

Assessment: Source analysis with supported arguments and evidence

üå°Ô∏è Activity 4: Desert Survival Science

Objective: Explore scientific principles of desert survival and adaptation

Materials: Thermometers, water testing kits, preservation materials

Process:

  1. Investigate water conservation and purification techniques
  2. Study temperature regulation and heat adaptation strategies
  3. Test food preservation methods used by medieval travelers
  4. Design survival kits based on scientific principles

Assessment: Scientific investigation reports with data analysis

üèõÔ∏è Activity 5: Medieval African Civilizations

Objective: Analyze the complexity and achievements of medieval African kingdoms

Materials: Historical sources, maps, cultural artifacts (images)

Process:

  1. Research the Mali, Songhai, and Ghana empires
  2. Analyze political, economic, and social structures
  3. Study the role of Islam in West African societies
  4. Create presentations on African contributions to world civilization

Assessment: Research presentations with historical analysis and evidence

üìä Integrated Assessment System

üéØ Multi-Subject Assessment Approach

Assessment integrates ELA, science, and social studies standards through authentic tasks that mirror real-world applications of historical analysis, scientific investigation, and cross-cultural understanding.

üìã Portfolio-Based Assessment Components

  • Primary Source Analysis Essays (25%): Critical reading and argumentative writing using Ibn Battuta's accounts
  • Scientific Investigation Reports (25%): Desert survival, navigation, and environmental science
  • Geographic Analysis Projects (20%): Route mapping and environmental factors
  • Cultural Research Presentations (15%): Medieval African civilizations and Islamic influence
  • Mathematical Trade Analysis (15%): Economic calculations and statistical reasoning

üìù Rubric Categories

Exceeds Standards (4): Demonstrates deep understanding with sophisticated analysis and clear connections between subjects

Meets Standards (3): Shows solid understanding with accurate analysis and some cross-curricular connections

Approaching Standards (2): Displays basic understanding with simple analysis and limited connections

Below Standards (1): Shows minimal understanding with unclear analysis and no connections

üéØ Performance Task: Desert Caravan Expedition Plan

Scenario: You are organizing a 14th-century caravan expedition from Morocco to Mali. Create a comprehensive expedition plan that demonstrates your understanding of geography, science, economics, and cultural factors.

Required Components:

  1. Route Analysis: Detailed map with geographical challenges and seasonal considerations
  2. Scientific Preparations: Water management, navigation methods, and survival strategies
  3. Economic Planning: Trade goods analysis, cost-benefit calculations, and profit projections
  4. Cultural Considerations: Diplomatic strategies and cross-cultural communication plans
  5. Historical Evidence: Support all decisions with evidence from Ibn Battuta and other sources

Presentation Format: 12-minute presentation with visual aids, maps, and Q&A session

ü§ù Peer Review & Collaboration

Students engage in structured peer review processes, providing feedback on each other's historical analysis and collaborating on group projects that require diverse perspectives on medieval African civilizations.
